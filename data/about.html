Introduction to the Elbow Lake Interpretive App

Welcome to the Elbow Lake Environmental Education Centre and surrounding Elbow Lake property! These lands are managed as a protected nature reserve by our conservation partner, the Nature Conservancy of Canada. Enjoy your visit to the trails, but please abide by the following:

  • Register your intent to visit in advance by contacting the ELEEC by e-mail or by phone at 613-353-7968.
  • Do not bring any vehicles onto the property unless authorized to do so.
  • Leash your pet(s) at all times while on the trails, and take responsibility by cleaning up after them. Pets are not allowed on the beach.
  • Absolutely no entry in any buildings unless authorized to do so.
  • Pack-in / pack-out all garbage.
  • Do not intentionally disturb or remove any plant or living creature from the property.

All photos are © Queen's University Biological Station (QUBS) except where noted.

This Elbow Lake Trail Guide interpretive app was developed to introduce self-guiding visitors to a selection of interesting features of the Elbow Lake property, falling within three main themes: Environmental, Cultural and Research.

Each interpretive station is identified in three ways:

  1. By a coloured marker at the station site, stenciled to distinguish Environmental, Cultural and Research-themed stations;
  2. Visually overlain on the Trails Map, with a written description of the site in the app text; and
  3. Georeferenced, meaning each station is associated by its location in physical space, in this case by UTM coordinates in NAD83. Each station’s coordinates are included with the app text.

Virtual Geocaching

Each interpretive station is georeferenced at the top of their respective sections in the app. Input these waypoints into your GPS unit, and use them to navigate precisely to each station. Please respect that the Elbow Lake property is managed as a nature reserve, and remain on designated trails; as well, be mindful of the privacy of other guests registered to the ELEEC.

At each station, take the quiz to test your knowledge about each feature of interest. This shouldn’t be too hard if you are at the right location, and have paid close attention and given some thought to the associated interpretive text. Good luck and have fun!

The Elbow Lake property is centrally located within a unique area of the province known as the Frontenac Arch, characterized by a granite “bridge” connecting the Canadian Shield of the north to the Adirondack Mountains to the south. Here, north meets south while the limestone plains influence from the east and west – with the result being an incredible diversity of living things in a relatively small area. The Elbow Lake property helps protect that diversity in species, habitats and landscapes.

Although by no means pristine, the Elbow Lake property has managed to remain relatively free of the harmful non-native species that are invading other natural areas. Learn to recognize the invasive aliens that have made an appearance on the property, and please report them if found! As you will learn, some of these risk having serious negative effects on the natural biodiversity of the Elbow Lake property.

The Elbow Lake property has been protected as a nature reserve since 2008, a designation that will continue in perpetuity. In the recent past, however, the terrestrial and aquatic resources of the property were exploited for timber, food, recreation, and human occupation. From the ground, evidence of human impact is presented in relatively small pieces – but to really appreciate their effects, one must assume a bird’s eye view. These stations tell the story of individual features from the perspective of their role within the broader landscape.

The Queen’s University Biological Station has operated its headquarters on Lake Opinicon since 1946, supporting an incredible number and diversity of research projects carried out on QUBS properties and adjacent lands. The Elbow Lake property is host to a few long-term studies, some of which are led by Queen’s University researchers and others that are volunteer-driven Citizen Science, but all of these are contributing to scientific knowledge of animal populations and their adaptations to a changing environment.

While biodiversity exploration is encouraged on the Elbow Lake property, users interested in carrying out research are required to obtain all required permits in advance of their study. Wildlife or plant collection by any individual must be authorized by the appropriate agencies.
